# Productivity Metrics

## Overview

Productivity metrics measure how efficiently work gets completed, how smoothly it flows through a process, and whether the team is delivering value without excessive delay or waste. They are useful in engineering, operations, support, knowledge work, and workflow automation [web:243][web:244][web:249][web:251].

## Core metrics

| Metric | What it measures | Why it matters | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Throughput | Number of work items completed in a period. | Core output measure. | Useful for teams and systems [web:244][web:251]. |
| Velocity | Amount of work completed in a sprint or time box. | Delivery speed. | Common in agile teams [web:157]. |
| Cycle Time | Time from work start to completion. | Shows how quickly work is finished. | Lower is usually better [web:243][web:244][web:248]. |
| Lead Time | Time from request entering the queue to delivery. | Measures end-to-end responsiveness. | Includes waiting and queue time [web:243][web:244][web:248]. |
| Flow Efficiency | Share of total time spent actively working vs waiting. | Reveals waste in a process. | Higher is better [web:249]. |
| WIP | Number of items actively in progress. | Helps detect overload and bottlenecks. | Too much WIP slows flow. |
| Backlog Size | Number of work items waiting to be done. | Shows queue pressure. | Large backlog can signal delays. |
| Utilization Rate | Share of available time spent on work. | Resource usage efficiency. | High utilization can still hide bottlenecks [web:157][web:244]. |
| Task Completion Rate | Percentage of tasks completed. | Indicates execution reliability. | Useful for workflow products. |
| Completion Time | Time needed to finish a task. | Shows task speed. | Useful at task and process level. |
| First Response Time | Time to first response in a workflow. | Measures responsiveness. | Important in support and collaboration tools. |
| Resolution Time | Time to resolve a request or ticket. | Measures operational speed. | Important in service workflows. |
| On-Time Delivery Rate | Work completed by the expected deadline. | Measures planning reliability. | Strong for operations and project teams. |
| SLA Compliance Rate | Work completed within SLA. | Measures service performance. | Important in support and enterprise teams. |
| Rework Rate | Work that needs to be redone or corrected. | Shows inefficiency and quality issues. | Lower is better. |
| Error Rate | Mistakes per unit of work. | Reveals quality problems. | Affects productivity downstream. |
| Handoff Time | Time lost between teams or workflow stages. | Highlights bottlenecks. | Critical in cross-functional work. |
| Automation Rate | Percentage of work completed automatically. | Measures productivity leverage. | Important for AI and workflow products. |
| Self-Service Rate | Users resolving tasks without human help. | Shows efficiency and scale. | Important in support and internal tools. |
| Search-to-Action Rate | Searches that lead to completed work. | Shows usefulness of productivity tools. | Good for knowledge systems. |
| Knowledge Reuse Rate | Reuse of templates, docs, or assets. | Measures leverage from prior work. | Useful in internal collaboration tools. |
| Decision Turnaround Time | Time to make a decision. | Shows approval speed. | Important in management and operations. |
| Escalation Rate | Number of issues that need escalation. | Reveals workflow friction. | Useful in support and service teams. |
| Focus Time | Uninterrupted time available for deep work. | Important for knowledge worker effectiveness. | Often measured weekly [web:245][web:247][web:250]. |
| Meeting Load | Time spent in meetings. | Helps detect context-switching overhead. | High meeting load can reduce deep work [web:247][web:250]. |

## How to read these metrics

| Metric type | What it tells you |
|---|---|
| Output metrics | How much work was completed. |
| Flow metrics | How quickly work moves through the system. |
| Quality metrics | Whether the work is correct and reliable. |
| Resource metrics | How efficiently people and tools are used. |
| Health metrics | Whether the team has enough focus and sustainable capacity. |

## Common relationships

| Relationship | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Throughput and Cycle Time | More throughput with lower cycle time usually means better flow. |
| Lead Time and WIP | High WIP often increases lead time. |
| Utilization and Focus Time | High utilization may reduce focus time. |
| Meeting Load and Productivity | Too many meetings can reduce deep work and output. |
| Automation Rate and Throughput | More automation can increase throughput if the workflow is well designed. |

## Example interpretation

| Metric | Good signal | Bad signal |
|---|---|---|
| Throughput | More work completed consistently. | Output is falling. |
| Cycle Time | Short and stable. | Long or unpredictable. |
| Lead Time | Fast delivery from request to completion. | Long waiting periods. |
| Flow Efficiency | More time spent actively working. | Too much waiting. |
| Focus Time | Long blocks of uninterrupted work. | Fragmented workdays. |
| Meeting Load | Reasonable and purposeful meetings. | Excessive context switching. |

## Use cases

| Product or team type | Most useful metrics |
|---|---|
| Engineering teams | Throughput, Cycle Time, Lead Time, Rework Rate, SLA Compliance |
| Operations teams | Backlog Size, Resolution Time, Decision Turnaround Time, Escalation Rate |
| Support teams | First Response Time, Resolution Time, CSAT, SLA Compliance |
| Workflow tools | Task Completion Rate, Automation Rate, Search-to-Action Rate |
| Knowledge workers | Focus Time, Meeting Load, Knowledge Reuse Rate, Decision Turnaround Time |

## Practical tips

| Tip | Why it helps |
|---|---|
| Track flow, not just output | A team can look busy but still be inefficient. |
| Combine productivity with quality | Faster work is not useful if quality drops. |
| Watch WIP carefully | Too much work in progress slows delivery. |
| Use consistent definitions | Cycle time, lead time, and throughput must be defined clearly. |
| Segment by team or workflow | Different teams can have different bottlenecks. |

## Interview use

In interviews, define productivity metrics as signals of how efficiently work moves through a system. Then explain the difference between output, flow, quality, and health metrics, and use cycle time, lead time, throughput, and focus time as examples.
