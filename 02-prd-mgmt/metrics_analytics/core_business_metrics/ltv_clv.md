# LTV / CLV

## Definition

LTV (Lifetime Value) and CLV (Customer Lifetime Value) measure how much value a customer generates over the full relationship with a business. In most product and business contexts, the terms are used interchangeably [web:201][web:206].

## Simple formula

| Formula type | Formula | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| Simple revenue view | Average purchase value x purchase frequency x customer lifespan | Physical products, retail, repeat purchase businesses [web:198][web:200]. |
| SaaS / subscription view | ARPU x Gross Margin ÷ Churn Rate | Subscription and digital products [web:206]. |
| Profit view | Revenue-based LTV x Gross Margin | When you want profit, not just revenue [web:198][web:201]. |

## How to think about it

| Step | Question | Output |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | How much does the customer spend each time? | Average purchase value or ARPU. |
| 2 | How often do they buy or pay? | Purchase frequency or monthly subscription frequency. |
| 3 | How long do they stay? | Customer lifespan or retention period. |
| 4 | How much margin do you keep? | Gross margin or contribution margin. |
| 5 | What is the final customer value? | LTV / CLV. |

## Simple examples

### 1) Physical product example: Starbucks coffee

Assume:
- Average purchase = $6.
- Customer buys 3 times per week.
- Customer stays for 2 years.
- Gross margin = 60%.

Calculation:
- Weekly spend = $6 x 3 = $18.
- Yearly spend = $18 x 52 = $936.
- 2-year revenue = $936 x 2 = $1,872.
- CLV = $1,872 x 60% = $1,123.20.

| Item | Value |
|---|---:|
| Average purchase value | $6 |
| Purchase frequency | 3 times/week |
| Customer lifespan | 2 years |
| Revenue CLV | $1,872 |
| Profit CLV | $1,123.20 |

### 2) Digital product example: paid iPhone app

Assume:
- App price = $4.99 per month.
- Average customer stays for 10 months.
- Gross margin = 85%.

Calculation:
- Revenue LTV = $4.99 x 10 = $49.90.
- Profit CLV = $49.90 x 85% = $42.42.

| Item | Value |
|---|---:|
| Monthly price | $4.99 |
| Lifespan | 10 months |
| Revenue LTV | $49.90 |
| Profit CLV | $42.42 |

### 3) Financial product example: credit card

Assume:
- Annual fee + interchange revenue per customer = $120 per year.
- Average customer stays for 5 years.
- Gross margin = 70%.

Calculation:
- Revenue CLV = $120 x 5 = $600.
- Profit CLV = $600 x 70% = $420.

| Item | Value |
|---|---:|
| Annual revenue per customer | $120 |
| Customer lifespan | 5 years |
| Revenue CLV | $600 |
| Profit CLV | $420 |

## SaaS-style example

Assume:
- ARPU = $25/month.
- Gross margin = 80%.
- Monthly churn = 5%.

Calculation:
- LTV = ($25 x 80%) ÷ 5%.
- LTV = $20 ÷ 0.05 = $400.

| Item | Value |
|---|---:|
| ARPU | $25 |
| Gross margin | 80% |
| Churn rate | 5% |
| LTV | $400 |

## Why it matters

| Use case | Why LTV / CLV is useful |
|---|---|
| Acquisition | Helps decide how much CAC is acceptable. |
| Retention | Shows the value of keeping customers longer. |
| Pricing | Helps test whether pricing supports profitability. |
| Segmentation | Shows which customer groups are most valuable. |
| Product strategy | Helps prioritize features that increase repeat usage and lifespan. |

## Good rules of thumb

| Rule | Meaning |
|---|---|
| LTV should be greater than CAC | Otherwise growth may be unprofitable. |
| Higher retention usually increases LTV | Small churn improvements can have large impact. |
| Margin matters | Revenue alone can overstate value. |
| Different segments have different LTVs | Always calculate by cohort or segment. |
| Use real customer data when possible | Assumptions can mislead. |

## Practical tips

| Tip | Why it helps |
|---|---|
| Use cohort analysis | Gives more accurate lifecycle estimates. |
| Split by segment | New users, premium users, and power users often differ greatly. |
| Use gross margin, not just revenue | Gives a truer business view. |
| Compare against CAC | Best way to judge efficiency. |
| Recalculate periodically | Customer behavior changes over time. |

## Interview use

In interviews, explain LTV / CLV as the long-term value of a customer and show how it connects to CAC, retention, and pricing. Then use one simple example to prove you can apply it, not just define it.
