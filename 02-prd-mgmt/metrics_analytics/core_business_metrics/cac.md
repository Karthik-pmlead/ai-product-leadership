# CAC

## Definition

CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) is the average cost of acquiring one new customer. It includes marketing, sales, and other acquisition-related expenses [web:208][web:211][web:217].

## Simple formula

| Formula type | Formula | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Simple CAC | Total acquisition cost ÷ Number of new customers acquired | Use when you want a straightforward view [web:208][web:214]. |
| Expanded CAC | (Marketing cost + sales wages + tools + services + overhead) ÷ New customers acquired | Use when you want a fuller cost picture [web:208]. |

## Example

Assume:
- Marketing spend = $20,000.
- Sales spend = $10,000.
- New customers acquired = 300.

Calculation:
- CAC = $30,000 ÷ 300 = $100.

| Item | Value |
|---|---:|
| Total acquisition cost | $30,000 |
| New customers acquired | 300 |
| CAC | $100 |

## Why it matters

| Use case | Why CAC matters |
|---|---|
| Growth | Shows whether acquisition is efficient. |
| Pricing | Helps test whether customer economics work. |
| Channel strategy | Helps compare paid and organic acquisition. |
| Product strategy | Helps see whether product-led growth is reducing acquisition cost. |

## Good rules of thumb

| Rule | Meaning |
|---|---|
| CAC should be lower than LTV | Otherwise the business may be unprofitable. |
| Track by channel | Paid, organic, referrals, partnerships may differ. |
| Track by segment | Different customer groups may cost different amounts. |
| Include full costs when possible | Marketing only can understate true CAC. |

## Interview use

In interviews, define CAC clearly, show the formula, and explain how it connects to LTV, payback period, and growth efficiency.
