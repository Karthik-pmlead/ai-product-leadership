# Situation-Complication-Resolution (SCR) Framework

## Overview
The SCR framework is a storytelling structure popularized by McKinsey & Company. It is the primary tool used by consultants to structure messy business insights into a logical, compelling, and actionable narrative. Unlike analytical models (which find answers), SCR is designed to **communicate** the answer and persuade an audience to take action.

It is highly effective for product managers pitching new features, executives providing organizational updates, or consultants presenting final recommendations.

## Structural Breakdown
    ┌─────────────┐
    │ 1. SITUATION │ (Accepted Baseline Facts, Context)
    └──────┬──────┘
           ▼
    ┌─────────────┐
    │ 2. COMPLICATION │ (The Change, Problem, or Conflict)
    └──────┬──────┘
           ▼
    ┌─────────────┐
    │ 3. RESOLUTION │ (The Solution, Recommendation, or Plan)
    └─────────────┘

## Detailed Pillars

### 1. Situation (Setting the Scene)
* **Definition:** The current, undeniable reality. This section provides necessary context but contains **no controversy, no opinions, and no new insights**.
* **Objective:** To get the audience nodding in agreement by grounding them in shared facts (e.g., industry history, current state of business, agreed-upon goals).
* **Key Question:** What is the established, stable state of affairs?

### 2. Complication (Identifying the Conflict)
* **Definition:** The event, tension, or challenge that disrupts the stable *Situation*. This is the catalyst that makes the audience pay attention. It defines **why things must change**.
* **Components:** This can be an unexpected market entry, a tech shift, decreasing revenue, a regulatory change, or a competitive threat.
* **Key Question:** What went wrong, what changed, or what imminent threat is disrupting the baseline?

### 3. Resolution (Providing the Solution)
* **Definition:** The proposed recommendation, plan, or action that solves the *Complication* and restores stability (or achieves a superior state). This must be a direct answer to the conflict.
* **Components:** This section must include actionable, high-level steps and the associated value proposition or expected impact.
* **Key Question:** What must the organization do now to overcome the problem?

## SCR Example: A Tech Product Launch

* **SITUATION:** Our flagship RecSys (Recommendation System) has successfully increased user engagement by 15% annually for the past three years, cementing our market leadership.
* **COMPLICATION:** However, recent privacy-focused operating system updates have crippled our access to third-party data, rendering our core algorithm 40% less effective in key conversion metrics.
* **RESOLUTION:** Therefore, we must immediately pivot our R&D roadmap to integrate context-aware, first-party data capture models within our RecSys stack, reclaiming our algorithm's performance before competitors exploit this gap.

## Implementation Guide for Product Managers
SCR is vital for defining the *why* of a product:

1.  **Product Vision Deck:** Use SCR to introduce the market opportunity (Complication) and your product concept (Resolution).
2.  **Product Requirements (PRDs):** Frame the problem statement in the SCR format to align your engineering and design teams.
3.  **Stakeholder Updates:** Use SCR to rapidly summarize project pivots, resource conflicts, or competitive shifts.
