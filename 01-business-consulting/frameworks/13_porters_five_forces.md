# Porter's Five Forces Framework

## Overview
Developed by Michael Porter, this framework evaluates the structural attractiveness and long-term profit potential of an industry by examining five distinct micro-economic forces. It moves beyond direct competitors to evaluate the baseline power dynamics of a market.

### 1. Competitive Rivalry
* **Focus:** The intensity of competition among existing players in the market.
* **High if:** Industry growth is slow, fixed costs are high (causing price dumping), exit barriers are steep, or products are highly commoditized.

### 2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers
* **Focus:** The leverage suppliers hold over input prices, quality, and terms.
* **High if:** The supplier industry is highly concentrated, switching costs to alternative suppliers are punitive, or no viable substitutes exist for the component.

### 3. Bargaining Power of Buyers (Customers)
* **Focus:** The leverage customers hold to drive down prices or demand higher quality.
* **High if:** Buyers purchase in massive volumes, switching costs to competitors are near zero, or the industry's products are standardized/undifferentiated.

### 4. Threat of New Entrants
* **Focus:** The ease with which new competitors can enter the space and disrupt incumbents.
* **High if:** Capital requirements are low, economies of scale are minimal, customer loyalty is weak, or regulatory barriers are absent.

### 5. Threat of Substitutes
* **Focus:** The risk of alternative products outside the traditional competitive boundary meeting the same consumer need (e.g., trains substituting for regional flights).
* **High if:** The substitute offers an attractive price-to-performance trade-off or buyer switching costs are low.

## Key Case Interview Questions
* What are the primary structural barriers protecting incumbents in this industry?
* Are macro technological shifts lowering buyer switching costs or enabling alternative substitutes?
* How concentrated is the supplier layer, and can we integrate backward to mitigate supplier risk?
