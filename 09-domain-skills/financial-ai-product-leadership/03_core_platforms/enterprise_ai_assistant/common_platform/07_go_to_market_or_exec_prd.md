# Enterprise AI Assistant

## Go-To-Market & Execution PRD

---

# 1. Executive Summary

The Enterprise AI Assistant is not a standalone product.

It is an **enterprise transformation platform** that must be deployed, adopted, and scaled across multiple business units.

Success depends less on model capability and more on:

* Distribution strategy
* Workflow embedding
* Change management
* Trust building
* Incremental rollout design

This document defines how the platform is operationalized inside a large financial institution.

---

# 2. GTM Strategy Overview

The adoption strategy follows a **land-and-expand enterprise model**.

```text id="gtm1"
Single High-Value Function
        ↓
Expand to Adjacent Teams
        ↓
Enterprise-Wide Adoption
        ↓
Platform Standardization
```

---

# 3. Entry Point Strategy (Wedge)

The first deployment must target **high-friction, high-frequency workflows**.

Ideal initial wedges:

---

## 3.1 Compliance & Risk Teams

Why:

* High documentation load
* High search overhead
* High compliance sensitivity

Use Cases:

* Policy Q&A
* Risk summarization
* Audit preparation

---

## 3.2 Operations Teams

Why:

* Structured workflows
* Repetitive tasks
* High automation potential

Use Cases:

* Ticket generation
* Workflow execution
* Exception handling

---

## 3.3 Front Office Assistants

Why:

* High information dependency
* High time pressure

Use Cases:

* Meeting preparation
* Client summaries
* Research retrieval

---

# 4. Expansion Strategy

After initial adoption, expand into:

---

## Phase 1: Horizontal Expansion

* Add more teams in same function
* Increase workflow coverage

---

## Phase 2: Cross-Functional Expansion

* Risk → Trading → Wealth → Operations
* Shared knowledge layer adoption

---

## Phase 3: Enterprise Standardization

* Replace fragmented tools
* Become default enterprise assistant

---

# 5. Deployment Model

---

## 5.1 Pilot Phase

* Small group (50–200 users)
* One function (e.g., compliance)
* Controlled environment

Success Criteria:

* High adoption (>60%)
* High trust score
* Low hallucination rate

---

## 5.2 Scale Phase

* Expand to 1,000+ users
* Multiple departments
* Integrate core systems

---

## 5.3 Enterprise Rollout

* Firm-wide deployment
* Embedded in all workflows
* Standard enterprise tool

# 11. Key Risks in Adoption

---

## 11.1 Resistance to Change

Mitigation:

* Embed into existing tools
* Minimize workflow disruption

---

## 11.2 Trust Deficit

Mitigation:

* Explainability
* Human approval loops

---

## 11.3 Over-Automation Risk

Mitigation:

* Gradual autonomy rollout
* Strict approval layers

---

## 11.4 Data Silos

Mitigation:

* Unified knowledge layer
* Standard connectors

---

# 12. Success Definition

The platform is successful when:

* Employees default to AI for knowledge retrieval
* Workflows are partially or fully AI-assisted
* Decision-making speed improves materially
* AI becomes embedded in daily enterprise operations

---

# 13. Strategic Outcome

The Enterprise AI Assistant transitions from:

> A tool employees occasionally use

to

> The primary interface for enterprise knowledge and workflows

---

# 14. Final Recommendation

Adopt a phased, wedge-driven rollout strategy starting with high-friction operational teams, expand through adjacent functions, and ultimately establish the Enterprise AI Assistant as the default intelligence layer across the organization.

This approach maximizes:

* Adoption speed
* Trust building
* Risk control
* Long-term scalability

---

# Closing Statement

Enterprise AI succeeds not through model capability alone, but through **careful integration into how people actually work**.

This GTM strategy ensures the assistant becomes not just a product—but an institutional capability.
